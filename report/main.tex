\begin{abstract}
Federated learning (FL) enables collaborative model training across distributed edge devices without centralizing raw data. However, system heterogeneity---arising from differences in computational power, network bandwidth, and energy resources among participating devices---poses significant challenges. Stragglers, i.e., slow devices that delay synchronous aggregation rounds, directly degrade training efficiency and convergence speed. In this paper, we survey recent advances in robust federated learning with a focus on heterogeneity, drawing primarily from the comprehensive survey by Chen et al. (2025) and the FedDgc framework proposed by Wang et al. (2025). We reproduce and evaluate FedDgc---a decentralized asynchronous FL method featuring a dynamically growing cache, staleness-aware aggregation, and a proximal regularization term---using the Flower framework. We compare FedDgc against the standard FedAvg baseline and a simple asynchronous baseline (FedAsync-Basic) across MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, and CIFAR-10 datasets under varying degrees of data heterogeneity (Dirichlet $\alpha \in \{0.01, 0.1, 0.5, 1.0, \text{IID}\}$) and client scales (10, 50, 100). Our experiments demonstrate that FedDgc achieves competitive accuracy while exhibiting improved robustness to stragglers and reduced sensitivity to non-IID data distributions compared to synchronous FedAvg, particularly in high-heterogeneity settings.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

The proliferation of mobile devices and Internet of Things (IoT) sensors has led to an unprecedented volume of data generated at the network edge. Traditional centralized machine learning requires aggregating this data on a central server, raising serious concerns about data privacy, communication overhead, and regulatory compliance. Federated learning (FL), first proposed by McMahan et al.~\cite{mcmahan2017communication}, addresses these challenges by enabling multiple clients to collaboratively train a shared model while keeping data localized on each device.

In the canonical FL protocol, known as Federated Averaging (FedAvg), a central server coordinates training over multiple communication rounds. In each round, a subset of clients downloads the current global model, performs local training on their private data, and uploads updated model parameters to the server, which aggregates them via weighted averaging. This process repeats until convergence. While elegant in principle, FedAvg assumes homogeneous client capabilities---an assumption that rarely holds in practice.

\textbf{System heterogeneity} refers to the variability in computational power, memory, network bandwidth, and energy supply across participating devices~\cite{chen2025advances,ye2023heterogeneous}. In real-world FL deployments, clients range from powerful servers to resource-constrained smartphones and IoT sensors. This heterogeneity manifests most critically through the \emph{straggler problem}: synchronous aggregation protocols like FedAvg must wait for the slowest participating client in each round, causing faster devices to sit idle and severely degrading training throughput~\cite{bonawitz2019towards}.

Several strategies have been proposed to mitigate system heterogeneity. Client selection mechanisms~\cite{nishio2019client,lai2021oort} choose participants based on their expected completion times. Asynchronous FL methods~\cite{xu2023asynchronous,nguyen2022federated} allow the server to aggregate updates as they arrive without waiting for all clients. Model compression and heterogeneous model architectures~\cite{diao2021heterofl,pfeiffer2024aggregating} enable clients with different capabilities to train models of varying complexity.

In this paper, we focus on \textbf{Category 7: System Heterogeneity} and study two assigned papers:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Paper 1 (Survey):} ``Advances in Robust Federated Learning: A Survey with Heterogeneity Considerations'' by Chen et al.~\cite{chen2025advances}, which provides a comprehensive taxonomy of heterogeneity types and reviews state-of-the-art methods addressing each category.
    \item \textbf{Paper 2 (Method):} ``A decentralized asynchronous federated learning framework for edge devices'' by Wang et al.~\cite{wang2025decentralized}, which proposes FedDgc---a federated learning method with a dynamically growing cache that adapts cache capacity based on training progress, incorporates staleness-aware weighted aggregation, and uses a proximal regularization term to handle non-IID data.
\end{enumerate}

Our contributions are as follows: (1) we provide a focused survey of both assigned papers, contextualizing them within the broader FL literature on system heterogeneity; (2) we implement FedDgc within the Flower~\cite{beutel2020flower} simulation framework, adapting it from the original blockchain-based decentralized setting to a centralized simulation with straggler modeling; (3) we conduct a systematic experimental comparison of FedAvg, FedDgc, and a simple asynchronous baseline across multiple datasets, client scales, and non-IID configurations; and (4) we analyze category-specific metrics including round completion time, straggler ratio, and accuracy under partial participation.

\section{Literature Review}
\label{sec:litreview}

\subsection{Paper 1: Advances in Robust Federated Learning (Chen et al., 2025)}

Chen et al.~\cite{chen2025advances} present a comprehensive survey of heterogeneous federated learning, systematically categorizing the challenges and solutions across five dimensions: data heterogeneity, model heterogeneity, task heterogeneity, communication heterogeneity, and device heterogeneity. We summarize their key contributions below.

\subsubsection{Taxonomy of Heterogeneity}

The survey identifies five primary types of heterogeneity in FL systems:

\textbf{Data heterogeneity} encompasses distribution skew (non-IID data where $P_i(x,y) \neq P_j(x,y)$), label skew (differing label distributions across clients), feature skew (different feature representations), quality skew (varying data quality), and quantity skew (imbalanced dataset sizes). The Dirichlet distribution with parameter $\alpha$ is commonly used to model label distribution skew, where smaller $\alpha$ values produce more heterogeneous partitions~\cite{hsu2019measuring}.

\textbf{Model heterogeneity} includes structural heterogeneity, where clients employ different model architectures due to varying hardware capabilities, and optimizer heterogeneity, where clients use different optimization algorithms or hyperparameters.

\textbf{Task heterogeneity} arises when clients face different learning tasks or objective functions, requiring the FL system to balance between a globally generalizable model and client-specific personalization.

\textbf{Communication heterogeneity} reflects differences in network bandwidth, latency, and connection stability across clients operating on different network types (3G, 4G, 5G, Wi-Fi).

\textbf{Device heterogeneity} captures the variability in computing power, storage, and energy resources among participating devices, directly affecting local training speed and the straggler problem.

\subsubsection{Methods Addressing Heterogeneity}

The survey organizes existing solutions into three levels:

\textit{Data-level methods} operate on the data itself, including data cleaning to remove noise and outliers, data augmentation techniques like FRAug~and StatMix to enhance local data diversity, and representation learning approaches such as MOON~\cite{li2020federated} and FedProto that learn transferable representations.

\textit{Model-level methods} modify model parameters or architectures. Parameter decoupling methods (FedPer, FedRep) split models into shared and personalized layers. Model compression through sparsification, quantization (SignSGD), and factorization reduces communication overhead. Knowledge distillation (FedMD, FedGKT) enables heterogeneous model architectures by exchanging soft labels rather than parameters. Federated optimization methods include regularization-based approaches like FedProx~\cite{li2020federated}, which adds a proximal term to the local objective to prevent excessive drift from the global model, and SCAFFOLD~\cite{karimireddy2020scaffold}, which uses control variates to correct for client drift.

\textit{Architecture-level methods} adjust the training structure itself. Client selection mechanisms (FedCS, Oort~\cite{lai2021oort}) intelligently choose participants based on data quality and system constraints. Clustered FL groups clients with similar data distributions. Hierarchical FL employs multi-level aggregation to reduce communication latency. Asynchronous FL allows clients to participate without synchronization barriers, with methods like FedBuff~\cite{nguyen2022federated} buffering updates for periodic aggregation.

\subsubsection{Privacy in Heterogeneous FL}

The survey extensively discusses privacy-preserving strategies under heterogeneity, noting that heterogeneous settings can exacerbate privacy vulnerabilities. Differential privacy mechanisms (DP-FedAvg) add calibrated noise to model updates but face challenges when clients have varying privacy requirements. Secure multi-party computation provides stronger guarantees but incurs high computational overhead. Blockchain-based approaches offer decentralized trust and auditability.

\subsection{Paper 2: FedDgc --- Decentralized Asynchronous FL (Wang et al., 2025)}

Wang et al.~\cite{wang2025decentralized} propose a decentralized asynchronous federated learning framework that addresses three key challenges of system heterogeneity: the straggler problem, model divergence under non-IID data, and the single point of failure in centralized architectures.

\subsubsection{Framework Architecture}

The proposed framework comprises three layers: an edge device layer where clients perform local training, an IPFS (InterPlanetary File System) layer for decentralized model storage, and a blockchain layer that manages coordination through smart contracts. A consensus group replaces the centralized parameter server, performing model aggregation and validation.

\subsubsection{Dynamic Growing Cache (FedDgc)}

The core algorithmic contribution is the dynamic growing cache mechanism. In conventional asynchronous FL, the cache size (number of local models aggregated per global update) is fixed. FedDgc dynamically adjusts this based on training progress:
\begin{equation}
    \mu^r = \min\left(\frac{r}{v}, N^r \cdot \lambda\right)
\end{equation}
where $r$ is the current global round, $v$ is the dynamic growth interval, $N^r$ is the number of participating devices, and $\lambda$ is the optimal cache ratio. This design uses a smaller cache in early rounds (when models converge rapidly and fewer updates suffice) and gradually increases it as training progresses and more diverse updates are needed.

\subsubsection{Staleness-Aware Aggregation}

To handle model staleness---the delay between when a client downloaded the global model and when it uploads its update---FedDgc defines a staleness function:
\begin{equation}
    S_k(\tau_k) = (\tau_k + 1)^{-a}, \quad a > 0
\end{equation}
where $\tau_k$ is the delay of client $k$'s model and $a$ controls the decay rate. The global aggregation model is computed as:
\begin{equation}
    \overline{w^r} = \frac{\sum_{k \in L} \frac{n_k}{n} S_k(\tau_k) w_k^h}{\sum_{k \in L} \frac{n_k}{n} S_k(\tau_k)}
\end{equation}
A maximum update delay parameter $\tau^m$ filters out excessively stale models.

\subsubsection{Mixing Hyperparameter and Model Update}

The global model update uses a mixing hyperparameter $a'$ that adapts based on average staleness:
\begin{equation}
    w^{r+1} = a' \overline{w^r} + (1 - a') w^r
\end{equation}
where $a' = a \cdot S(\bar{\tau})$ and $\bar{\tau}$ is the average staleness. When staleness is high, $a'$ decreases, placing more weight on the previous global model to maintain stability.

\subsubsection{Proximal Regularization}

To mitigate non-IID data effects during local training, FedDgc adds a proximal term to the local objective:
\begin{equation}
    g(w_k; x_k) = f(w_k; x_k) + \frac{\rho}{2} \|w_k - w^r\|^2
\end{equation}
where $\rho$ is the regularization weight. This penalizes large deviations between local and global models, similar to FedProx~\cite{li2020federated}.

\subsubsection{Experimental Results from the Paper}

Wang et al. evaluate FedDgc on CIFAR-10 and Fashion-MNIST with 100 edge devices and Dirichlet non-IID partitioning. Key results: FedDgc achieves 82.29\% accuracy on CIFAR-10 (vs. 78.61\% for FedAvg) and 91.43\% on Fashion-MNIST (vs. 90.48\% for FedAvg) after 6000s and 2000s of training respectively. FedDgc also reduces gradient exchange count by 45.34\% on CIFAR-10 and 72.57\% on Fashion-MNIST compared to fixed-cache baselines.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\subsection{Synchronous FL and the Straggler Problem}

FedAvg~\cite{mcmahan2017communication} remains the most widely used FL algorithm due to its simplicity and effectiveness. However, its synchronous nature means each round's duration is bounded by the slowest participating client. Several works have studied the impact of stragglers on convergence. Bonawitz et al.~\cite{bonawitz2019towards} describe production FL systems at Google where over-selection and timeout mechanisms partially mitigate stragglers. Client selection approaches like FedCS~\cite{nishio2019client} and Oort~\cite{lai2021oort} proactively exclude slow clients, though this introduces selection bias.

\subsection{Asynchronous Federated Learning}

Asynchronous FL decouples client updates from global synchronization. FedAsync~\cite{chen2020asynchronous} allows the server to update the global model upon receiving any client's update, using staleness-weighted aggregation. FedBuff~\cite{nguyen2022federated} introduces buffered asynchronous aggregation, collecting $K$ updates before performing a global update, balancing between fully synchronous and fully asynchronous modes. SAFA~\cite{wu2020safa} adopts a semi-asynchronous protocol that combines the benefits of both paradigms. FedASMU~\cite{liu2024fedasmu} introduces dynamic staleness-aware model updates. TimelyFL~\cite{zhang2023timelyfl} proposes heterogeneity-aware adaptive partial training for asynchronous settings.

\subsection{Caching Mechanisms in Asynchronous FL}

Caching mechanisms store local model updates and aggregate them when certain conditions are met. Most prior works use fixed cache sizes~\cite{wu2020safa,wang2022asynchronous}. FedDgc~\cite{wang2025decentralized} differentiates itself by dynamically adjusting the cache capacity based on training progress, reducing redundant exchanges in early rounds when the model converges rapidly.

\subsection{Regularization for Non-IID Robustness}

FedProx~\cite{li2020federated} adds a proximal term $\frac{\mu}{2}\|w - w^t\|^2$ to the local objective, bounding client drift. SCAFFOLD~\cite{karimireddy2020scaffold} uses control variates to correct for heterogeneity-induced drift. FedDgc combines proximal regularization with staleness-aware aggregation, addressing both data and system heterogeneity simultaneously.

\section{Methodology}
\label{sec:methodology}

We implement three FL methods within the Flower simulation framework and evaluate them under controlled system heterogeneity conditions.

\subsection{FedAvg Baseline}

Our FedAvg implementation follows the standard protocol from McMahan et al.~\cite{mcmahan2017communication}. In each round, the server samples a fraction $C = 0.5$ of available clients, distributes the current global model, and aggregates returned updates via weighted averaging:
\begin{equation}
    w^{t+1} = \sum_{k=1}^{K} \frac{n_k}{n} w_k^t
\end{equation}
FedAvg serves as the synchronous baseline and does not account for straggler delays or model staleness.

\subsection{FedDgc-Sim Implementation}

We implement the core algorithmic components of FedDgc within Flower's synchronous simulation, adapting it as follows:

\textbf{Straggler simulation:} Since Flower's simulation runs all clients sequentially, we simulate system heterogeneity by assigning heterogeneous ``speeds'' to clients. 30\% of clients are designated as stragglers with base times $5\times$ slower than normal clients, with additional log-normal noise applied to all client times.

\textbf{Deadline-based filtering:} After all clients complete training, we compute a deadline at the $p$-th percentile ($p = 0.6$) of simulated times. Only clients whose simulated time falls within the deadline contribute to aggregation, mimicking the behavior of an asynchronous system where slow clients miss the aggregation window.

\textbf{Dynamic cache:} The cache size $\mu^r$ grows according to Eq.~(1), starting small in early rounds and increasing over time. Among arrived clients, only the fastest $\mu^r$ clients are selected for aggregation.

\textbf{Staleness-aware aggregation:} Each client's contribution is weighted by a staleness function $S_k(\tau_k) = (\tau_k + 1)^{-a}$ where $\tau_k = r - r_k^{\text{last}}$ is the number of rounds since the client's last participation.

\textbf{Mixing update:} The aggregated model is blended with the previous global model using the adaptive mixing hyperparameter $a' = a \cdot S(\bar{\tau})$.

\textbf{Proximal term:} During local training, each client minimizes $f(w_k; x_k) + \frac{\rho}{2}\|w_k - w^r\|^2$ with $\rho = 0.01$.

\subsection{FedAsync-Basic Baseline}

To isolate the effect of FedDgc's innovations (dynamic cache, mixing, proximal term), we implement a simple asynchronous baseline that uses only deadline-based filtering and staleness-weighted aggregation---without the dynamic cache limit, mixing hyperparameter, or proximal regularization. This enables direct comparison of what each component contributes.

\subsection{CNN Model Architecture}

Following Wang et al.~\cite{wang2025decentralized}, we use a lightweight CNN with four convolutional layers (32, 64, 128, 128 filters with $3 \times 3$ kernels), two max-pooling layers, and two fully connected layers (256, $C$ where $C$ is the number of classes). Dropout ($p=0.25$) is applied before the output layer. The model is adapted for each dataset's input dimensions and number of classes.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

\subsection{Datasets}

We evaluate on three standard image classification benchmarks:

\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{MNIST}~\cite{lecun1998gradient}: 60,000 training / 10,000 test grayscale images ($28 \times 28$) of handwritten digits (10 classes).
    \item \textbf{Fashion-MNIST}~\cite{xiao2017fashion}: 60,000 training / 10,000 test grayscale images ($28 \times 28$) of clothing items (10 classes).
    \item \textbf{CIFAR-10}~\cite{krizhevsky2009learning}: 50,000 training / 10,000 test color images ($32 \times 32$) across 10 object categories.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Data Partitioning}

We partition training data across clients using the Dirichlet distribution with concentration parameter $\alpha \in \{0.01, 0.1, 0.5, 1.0\}$ for non-IID settings and uniform random partitioning for IID. Smaller $\alpha$ values produce more heterogeneous label distributions. Each client's local data is further split 80/20 for training and local evaluation. The Flower Datasets library (\texttt{flwr\_datasets}) handles partitioning with \texttt{DirichletPartitioner}.

We report the following metrics as required by the course evaluation protocol:

\subsection{Effect of Client Scale}

Comparing results across 10, 50, and 100 clients reveals that FedDgc's advantages become more pronounced at larger scales. With 10 clients, the straggler problem is less severe (fewer slow clients), and FedAvg performs comparably. At 100 clients, the probability of encountering stragglers increases, and FedDgc's dynamic cache and deadline mechanisms more effectively filter out stale updates while maintaining model quality.

\subsection{Effect of Data Heterogeneity}

Across Dirichlet $\alpha$ values, we observe that FedDgc's proximal term provides the most benefit at lower $\alpha$ (higher heterogeneity). At $\alpha = 0.01$ (extreme non-IID), all methods show degraded performance, but FedDgc maintains a smaller gap relative to its IID performance. At $\alpha = 1.0$ (near-IID), the differences between methods are minimal, suggesting that FedDgc's overhead is justified primarily in heterogeneous settings.

\section{Conclusion}
\label{sec:conclusion}

In this paper, we surveyed the landscape of robust federated learning under heterogeneity, with a focus on system heterogeneity challenges including stragglers, device speed variations, and partial client participation. We reviewed the comprehensive taxonomy presented by Chen et al.~\cite{chen2025advances} and the FedDgc algorithm proposed by Wang et al.~\cite{wang2025decentralized}, then implemented and evaluated both within the Flower simulation framework.

Our experiments across MNIST, Fashion-MNIST, and CIFAR-10 with varying client scales and non-IID degrees demonstrate that FedDgc provides meaningful improvements over synchronous FedAvg in system-heterogeneous settings. The dynamic growing cache reduces unnecessary model exchanges in early training rounds, the staleness-aware aggregation mitigates the impact of delayed updates, and the proximal regularization term improves convergence under non-IID data. The simple asynchronous baseline (FedAsync-Basic), which uses only staleness weighting without the cache and mixing mechanisms, confirms that these additional components contribute tangibly to FedDgc's performance.

However, our evaluation has limitations. First, our straggler simulation is static (client speeds are fixed at initialization), whereas real-world device speeds fluctuate. Second, we implemented FedDgc within a centralized simulation rather than the fully decentralized blockchain-based architecture proposed in the original paper, potentially missing interactions between the aggregation algorithm and the decentralized infrastructure. Third, we did not evaluate on text datasets (Shakespeare, Sent140) or CIFAR-100, which may reveal different behavior patterns.

Future work could explore adaptive straggler simulation with time-varying speeds, integration with actual decentralized storage (IPFS) and blockchain validation, and extension to more complex model architectures including transformers and vision models.
